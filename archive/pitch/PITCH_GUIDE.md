## 4. Slide Structure — Verbatim Twitter Thread

Source: Pitch advice thread for MONOLITH hackathon participants.

### Slide 1: The Hook

You have 15 seconds. If you don't grab attention with something novel, unique, or punchy:

The judges are gone.

Open with novelty: Be it humor, innovation, or simply being frank.

Judges sit through a lot of boring pitches, just don't be boring.

### Slide 2: The Problem

1. Define the problem you're solving,
2. Back it with data that proves the problem exists.
3. If you sell the problem well, judges are 100% locked in for the solution.

If they doubt the problem you define, the rest of your pitch is dead.

### Slide 3: Value Prop

This is the bridge between your problem and solution.

Take it from last year's winners:

@NOMADZxyz: "Crypto-friendly travel bookings"
@SP3NDdotshop: "Buy anything on Amazon with stablecoins."

It should be instantly obvious what you do for the user.

### Slide 4: How It Works

Show your app in 3 simple steps. Step 1, Step 2, Step 3.

No jargon. No architecture diagrams. Just: connect wallet -> do thing -> get result.

### Slide 5: The Demo

20-30 seconds max. Record your phone screen, embed it in the deck, voiceover what's happening.

No fancy animations. No music. Just real usage.

Bonus: build in a "wow moment" -- one thing that makes judges go "oh, that's cool."

### Slide 6: The Market

Forget the "if we capture 1% of a $50B market" slide.

Instead: show a specific user intersection. Who exactly are your users? How many exist? Do you have existing growth stats?

Consider adding relevant growth trends (stablecoin adoption, etc)

### Slide 7: Business Model

How do you capture value? Keep it dead simple.

Do you have continuity? Can this app survive as a business and be around next year?

Consider adding in a financial model slide that shows:

- How you'll make rev
- When you'll make a profit or break-even

### Slide 8: Traction

This is what judges actually want to see. Not what you built: what happened when real people used it.

Registered users > waitlist signups > X followers (don't fake em, bad look).

Show growth curves if you have them. Retention data is gold.

### Slide 9: Roadmap

Show where you are now (Q1 2026) and the next 2-3 quarters.

Every milestone needs a real date -- quarters, months, whatever. Not just "Phase 1, Phase 2."

This proves you have a plan and know where you're going.

### Slide 10: Team

Don't just list names and roles. Every team slide looks like that.

Highlight 1-2 facts only YOUR team can claim. "Built a game with 320K users." "Backed by Circle."

The message: we did it before. We'll do it again.

### Slide 11: Call to Action

Don't rush the ending. This is your last impression.

QR code to try your app. Link to a longer demo. A way to book a call.

Give judges a next step if they're interested. Make it easy.

### Overarching Advice

Remember: you're pitching a business, not a product.

The pitch is a teaser.

It exists to make judges want to know more: not to explain everything.

Stand out by being concise and easy to understand.

Good luck MONOLITH builders. Join the Discord & ping @KEMOS4BE for pitch help.

## 5. Language Rules — Verbatim Article

Source: Article on pitch language traps.

This is a tricky one. Cause if no one told you, it's almost impossible to catch by yourself.
Yet it's super common for builders to fall into sentence patterns (certain words, tenses, or phrasing) that unintentionally sabotage the edge they're trying to create with their best pitch.
Since I notice it in every single coaching session, let me break it down here:
What kills your pitch. And how to instantly fix it.
This article is not focusing on the pitch content itself, but really on the most common language traps.

### 1. Verbs to ban: trying, hoping, imagining

They quietly signal uncertainty and lack of control.

So what we are trying to do is...
- Trying feels unsure.
- Say: "We're doing..." or "We're building..."
- Investors and partners don't back triers, they back doers.

Hope to release our platform in Q4.
- Hope implies lack of planning or confidence.
- Say: "We're on track to release..." or "We're planning to launch..."

What we imagine for the future is..
- You don't imagine. You plan.
- Say: "Our vision is..." or "We're building toward..."

### 2. "would" "should" "could" "might"

Conditional form creates uncertainty, uncertainty destroys trust and attention.

This one is simple, any form based on odds instead of certainty is to ban right away. The fix is straightforward:
what we would / should / could / might do is => what we do is

For instance:
When you say:
"We would target enterprise clients..."
"This might be a $1B market..."
"We could scale this model..."
"Users should love this feature..."
You're unconsciously telling your audience: "I'm not sure." "This is just a theory."

The fix: certainty + action
Replace conditionals with confident, clear statements:
"We are targeting enterprise clients."
"This is a $1B market based on [data]."
"We can scale this model through [strategy]."
"Users love this feature, we've seen [result/stat]."

### 3. Fix your tenses

Present tense shows confidence. Future tense sounds like a wish.

Don't just pitch potential. Pitch momentum.

Remember, a pitch is the best version of your project. Well executed, it's you creating your own reality and inviting others to believe in it.

It's ok to go slightly beyond what's already live, as long as you're confident you can ship it fast.

Where to always use present and past tenses:
- value proposition "X is ..."
- product features "it connects to..."
- traction & market fit "we've achieved..."

The only parts where future tense is acceptable:
- roadmap
- growth projections

Adopt this rule strictly and I promise you your audience will listen twice as actively.
This advice is not about scoring points, it's about not losing them.
Let's go!

## 11. Presentation Tips

### For This Specific Hackathon
1. **Lead with the Spark face** — it's the X-factor
2. **Show the phone** — mobile hackathon, demonstrate mobile-native feel
3. **Name-drop integrations** — MWA, Seed Vault, Blinks, SOAR, Tapestry
4. **Be frank about solo dev** — it's impressive, not a weakness
5. **Don't oversell future plans** — show what IS, not what MIGHT be

### Delivery
1. Rehearse to under 3 minutes
2. Speak in present tense — "The Monolith IS..."
3. Pause after the hook — let it land
4. Demo > slides — if you can only do one, make it the demo

### Things to Avoid
- "We're trying to..." / "We hope to..." / "If we get 1% of..."
- Architecture diagrams (save for appendix)
- More than 5 bullets on any slide
- Reading from the slides
- Apologizing ("sorry this is just a prototype" — NO)
